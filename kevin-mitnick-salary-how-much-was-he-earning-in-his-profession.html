Early Life and Career

Kevin David Mitnick, born August 6, 1963, went on to become one of America’s most famous computer security consultants, authors, and convicted hackers.

He was arrested in 1995 and spent five years in jail for a bunch of computer and communications crimes.

His trial, trial, and prison sentence were controversial, and so were the reporting, books, and movies that followed.

After he got out, Mitnick started his own security firm called Mitnick Security Consulting LLC, and he also worked with other computer security firms.

Mitnick was 12 years old when he was told by a bus driver where he could get a ticket punch for a school project.

What was Kevin Mitnick salary before his death?

Mitnick’s first breach of a computer network occurred in 1979 when he was 16 years old.

A friend provided him with the phone number of the Ark, a computer system used by DEC to develop its RSTS / E operating system software.

Mitnick hacked into DEC’s computer network and copied DEC’s software, a felony offense for which he was convicted in 1988.

Mitnick served 12 months in prison and was on supervised release for three years.

Not long after his supervised release ended, Mitnick broke into Pacific Bell’s voicemail computers.

Mitnick fled the country and was on the run for two and a half years.

According to the US Justice Department, Mitnick accessed hundreds of computer networks illegally while on the run.

He cloned cell phones to mask his location and stole valuable software from major cell phone and computer companies in the US.

He also hacked into computers, stole passwords, changed networks, and hacked into private emails.

Following a widely publicized investigation, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) apprehended Mitnick at his residence in Raleigh, NC, on February 15th, 1995, for federal offenses relating to a period of two and a half years of computer hacking, including computer fraud and wire fraud, in which he was found to be in possession of cloned mobile telephones, more than one hundred cloned cellular telephone codes, and numerous false documents.

On December 17th, 1997, Yahoo!’s website was hacked and a message was displayed requesting Mitnick’s immediate release.

The message stated that all recent visitors to Yahoo! had been infected by a computer worm, which would cause a major disruption on Christmas Day if Mitnick was not removed.

Yahoo! denied the allegations, claiming that the worm did not exist.
